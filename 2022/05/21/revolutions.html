revolutions

May 21, 2022

internet

Every once in a while, civilization is radically transformed through technological revolutions:

  • Food production (Agricultural Revolution, ~8000 BC)
  • Industry (Industrial Revolution, ~1760)
  • Electrical power (Second Industrial Revolution, ~1880)
  • Internet (Information Revolution, ~1990)

The Information Revolution is the latest addition to this list and therefore the one whose full impact still remains to be felt. The internet allows for two (seemingly) contradictory trends to occur simultanously: increasing equality and increasing inequality. How can this be?

  • Increasing equality: An internet connection is the great equalizer when it comes to leveling the playing field in terms of access to information. This gives everyone the opportunity to educate themselves.
  • Increasing inequality: Equality of opportunity does not result in equality of outcome. Even though everyone has the same opportunity; the internet offers many rabit holes: some are more useful than others.

The impact of the internet has occured in waves, some notable ones:

  • web 1.0 (Amazon, Google, Paypal)
  • web 2.0 (Facebook, Youtube, Instagram)
    • Apps (Uber, Deliveroo, Tinder)

Web 1.0 was first wave of (centralized) internet services such as Amazon, Google and Paypal.

Web 2.0 was the first step towards a more peer-to-peer based form of human interconnection. Companies, such as Facebook, Youtube and Twitter, gave everyone a platform to express themselves, and replaced centralized information sources such as TV networks and newspapers. Some revolutions arguably wouldn’t have occured, were it not for this latest addition to humanity’s toolkit. Smartphones (and apps) have further increased this impact; by connecting everyone 24/7, new possibilities got unlocked: press a button, a cab arrives; press a button, dinner; press a button, a date. (No guarantees on that last one though)

Currently we are in the early stage of the third wave.

bitcoin

Bitcoin is a fundamental upgrade to the Internet Protocol: initially we got e-mail, now we have e-money. Going back to our previous discussion about the internet being a great equalizer in terms of access to information; one problem that still didn’t solve: lack of opportunity. Let’s imagine a child in the Amazon learns to program from Youtube videos. How would he monetize his skills? His village might not require many software engineers; paying him from across the internet in his local currency would also be pretty difficult. Now however, using Bitcoin, making a payment can be as easy as sending an e-mail.

The further bitcoinization of the world will occur slowly and then suddenly. It might seem like a small thing being able to send money, however in hindsight: e-mail was merely a prelude for what was to come. The consequences will be massive.

Goverments and banks are both instutions formed well before the rise of the internet, however have so far have been pretty unaffected by this massive change: they are now next in line for disruption. In my estimation most will be unable to adapt, and thus disintegrate or go bankrupt. The best examples of countries that are well-adopted to the coming changes: Singapore, UAE and Estonia; perhaps not coincidentally: all are relatively young. (Estonia is perhaps a post-internet country: restoring independence in 1991)

Bitcoin allows for the rise of completely new kinds of decentralized organizations. The first examples of remote-first companies have already started to show up (with some help from China). Bitcoin will only further accelerate this trend, erasing the arbitrary economic borders imposed by the hegemony of national currencies; leveling the economic playing field for everyone.

The creator of Bitcoin remains anonymous to this day.
